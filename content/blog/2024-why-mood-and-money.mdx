When people ask what the Mood & Money Lab studies, the link between financial stress and mental health often seems obvious in retrospect — and yet it is surprisingly under-studied with modern data science methods.

## The Problem in Plain Terms

Around **one in six working-age adults** in the UK experiences a mental health problem at any one time. At the same time, the Money and Mental Health Policy Institute estimates that **46% of people in problem debt** also have a mental health problem. These two crises are not just correlated: they fuel each other in a destructive cycle.

Financial stress triggers anxiety and depression. Anxiety and depression impair the executive function needed to manage money — budgeting, planning, resisting impulsive spending. Poor financial decisions worsen financial stress. The loop tightens.

Understanding *how* and *when* this cycle begins — and finding places to intervene — is the core motivation for our work.

## Why Now? The Role of AI and Digital Data

Three developments make this a particularly exciting moment for this research:

**1. Passively collected financial data at scale.** Open Banking regulations in the UK now enable consented, privacy-preserving research access to transaction data. For the first time, we can study financial behaviour continuously and objectively — not through surveys subject to recall bias, but through the actual digital trace of daily life.

**2. Large language models.** The latest generation of LLMs can read clinical notes, interview transcripts, and patient-written texts with remarkable comprehension. This opens doors for mental health screening at a scale that was previously impossible, potentially bridging the eleven-year gap between symptom onset and diagnosis.

**3. Real-time intervention capability.** Smartphones and banking apps sit in people's pockets throughout the day. If we can identify early warning signs of financial-mental health deterioration, we have an unprecedented opportunity to deliver personalised support at exactly the right moment.

## What We Are Careful About

None of this comes without responsibility. Predicting mental health from financial data raises serious questions about **privacy, consent, and the potential for discrimination** — for example, in credit scoring or insurance. Our research is built on three pillars:

- **Ethics first**: Every study begins with ethical review, data minimisation, and genuine participant co-design
- **Fairness by default**: We test all models for performance disparities across demographic groups before any publication or deployment
- **Open science**: We publish datasets, code, and model weights wherever possible, so findings can be scrutinised and built upon
