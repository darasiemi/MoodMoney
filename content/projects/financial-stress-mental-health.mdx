## Overview

Financial hardship is one of the strongest predictors of poor mental health, yet the causal mechanisms are poorly understood and rarely quantified at scale. This project analyses **anonymised, linked financial and mental health data** to answer three questions:

1. Can spending and income patterns predict deteriorating mental health before clinical presentation?
2. What financial behaviours are most strongly associated with psychological distress?
3. Can AI-powered financial coaching nudges improve mental wellbeing?

## Data

Working under a research data sharing agreement with a UK retail bank, we have access to:

- **2.3 million anonymised current accounts** with monthly transaction-level data (2018–2024)
- A subset of **45,000 accounts** where holders also completed validated mental health questionnaires (PHQ-9, GAD-7, WEMWBS) at 6-month intervals
- Linked social deprivation indices (Index of Multiple Deprivation) at LSOA level

All data handling follows NHS Data Security and Protection Toolkit standards and has full NHS REC ethical approval (ref: 23/XX/000).

## Machine Learning Pipeline

```
Transaction data → Feature engineering → Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost)
                                         ↓
                              Mental health score prediction (t+3 months)
                                         ↓
                              SHAP feature importance → Interpretable risk factors
```

**Key engineered features include:**
- Spending volatility (coefficient of variation across categories)
- Savings rate and trajectory
- Overdraft frequency and depth
- Late payment events
- Income regularity score

## Results

Our best model achieves:
- **AUROC 0.81** for predicting new-onset psychological distress at 3-month horizon
- **Top predictors**: overdraft events (14 days+), food spend drop >20%, irregular income
- Models are reasonably robust across age and gender, though performance drops for self-employed individuals (active area of improvement)

## Intervention Design

A second strand of this project designs and evaluates **AI-generated financial coaching messages** delivered within a banking app. Messages are personalised to spending patterns and framed using behavioural science principles.

We are currently running a **pre-registered RCT** (n=8,000) to evaluate the effect of these nudges on both financial behaviour and self-reported mental wellbeing at 3 and 6 months.

## Policy Impact

Findings have been presented to the **Money and Mental Health Policy Institute** and cited in their 2024 submission to HM Treasury. We are in dialogue with the FCA's Consumer Duty team about implications for vulnerability screening frameworks.
