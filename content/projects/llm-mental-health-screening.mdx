## Overview

Mental health conditions affect one in four people over their lifetime, yet the average time between symptom onset and clinical diagnosis exceeds **eleven years**. Reducing this gap is a central challenge in psychiatry — and one where AI can play a meaningful role.

This project investigates whether large language models (LLMs) can reliably assist clinicians in identifying depression and anxiety from unstructured text: clinical interview transcripts, electronic health records (EHRs), and patient-written self-reports.

## Research Questions

1. **Accuracy**: Can fine-tuned LLMs match or exceed PHQ-9 / GAD-7 agreement with clinician-rated diagnosis?
2. **Fairness**: Do models perform equitably across gender, age, ethnicity, and socioeconomic background?
3. **Explainability**: Can we generate clinician-interpretable rationales alongside model predictions?
4. **Safety**: Under what conditions should LLM-based screening be trusted versus escalated to human review?

## Methodology

We work with three data sources under appropriate ethical approvals:

- **MIMIC-IV**: De-identified clinical notes from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center
- **DepSeverity**: Social media text labelled against clinician ratings
- **MOSAIC**: A novel multi-modal dataset we are co-constructing with NHS partners, linking GP consultation transcripts to longitudinal mental health outcomes

Models evaluated include GPT-4o, Llama 3, Mistral 7B, and a purpose-built **ClinPsych-BERT** fine-tuned on psychiatric text.

We use a **fairness-constrained training** objective that penalises performance disparities across demographic subgroups, and evaluate explainability using SHAP-based attention visualisation.

## Key Findings So Far

- Fine-tuned LLMs achieve **AUROC 0.88** on depression detection from GP notes, versus 0.74 for zero-shot GPT-4o
- **Significant performance gaps** observed across ethnicity groups; mitigation through fairness-aware fine-tuning reduces this by ~40%
- Clinicians rated model-generated explanations as "useful" or "very useful" in 72% of cases in a pilot usability study
